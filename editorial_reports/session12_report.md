## Relevant Editorial Feedback (Mapped)
- **Missing learning objectives and context**: The chapter jumps straight into tasks without explaining what concept testing is or why it is important. Concept testing helps reduce risk by obtaining consumer feedback early in development【203286957744365†L147-L170】. Students need to know this context.
- **Undefined terms and survey methods**: Terms like “survey population,” “survey format,” and “communicate the concept” are not defined. Students need guidance on how to select a representative population, choose appropriate survey methods (face‑to‑face, online), and decide on communication formats (verbal, sketches, prototypes).
- **No explanation of sample size and market segmentation**: Students are told to determine sample size via external tools but are not given basic principles of sampling. There is also no discussion of how to identify relevant market segments for a hydroponic product.
- **Lack of survey design guidance**: The chapter lists typical concept test questions but does not instruct students on crafting effective questions or structuring a survey (e.g., screening questions, concept evaluation questions, purchase intent questions). Qualtrics emphasises that concept test questions should be designed to reduce launch risk by spotting problems early【203286957744365†L147-L170】.
- **No connection to previous sessions**: Students are not reminded to test the specific concepts selected in Session 11. The survey questions should be tailored to these concepts and to the needs identified earlier.
- **No advice on communication methods**: The list of communication methods is a bullet list with no guidance on choosing between them. Students need criteria (e.g., complexity of concept, cost, desired fidelity) to make informed choices.
- **Ambiguities and grammar issues**: Several instructions are awkwardly phrased (“The objective of this is activity is to better understand the importance of generating questions…”). Phrases should be clarified and simplified.
- **Missing conclusion**: The chapter does not summarise the purpose and expected outputs of concept testing or indicate how the results will be used in subsequent sessions.

## Action Plan (Prioritized)

### P0 – Must fix
- **Add learning objectives and introduction**: Introduce the concept of concept testing, emphasising that it is an early‑stage research method to obtain consumer feedback on ideas and reduce the risk of product failure【203286957744365†L147-L170】. List objectives: (1) define what to learn from the concept test, (2) select a representative survey population, (3) choose appropriate survey format, (4) draft effective survey questions, and (5) decide how to communicate concepts to respondents.
- **Define key terms and survey methods**: Provide concise definitions of “concept testing,” “survey population,” “market segment,” “sample size,” “survey format,” and communication methods (e.g., verbal description, sketch, prototype). Explain the differences between face‑to‑face interviews, telephone surveys, mail surveys, and online surveys, and discuss pros and cons.
- **Explain the purpose of the concept test**: Instruct students to identify specific research questions (e.g., Which concept do users prefer? How can the concept be improved? What is purchase intent?) and to tailor survey questions accordingly【203286957744365†L147-L170】. Provide examples relevant to the hydroponic concepts.
- **Provide guidelines for selecting survey populations and calculating sample sizes**: Explain how to identify the target market (e.g., hobby hydroponic growers vs. commercial growers), and how to select a survey population that reflects this segment. Offer basic guidance on determining sample size (e.g., using an online calculator with specified confidence level and margin of error) and emphasise representativeness.
- **Guide students in drafting survey questions**: Explain the structure of a concept test questionnaire: introduction, screening questions, concept presentation, evaluation questions (e.g., liking, purchase intent), follow‑up questions, and demographic questions. Provide examples. Highlight the importance of unbiased, clear wording.
- **Clarify communication methods**: Provide criteria for choosing how to present concepts (e.g., complexity, resources, fidelity, ability to convey key features). Give examples of when to use sketches versus physical prototypes. Encourage using the simplest method that effectively communicates the concept.

## Concrete Rewrite Instructions

### Chapter text
- **Add an introductory section** that defines concept testing, distinguishes it from product testing, and explains that concept testing reduces risk by obtaining user feedback early【203286957744365†L147-L170】. State learning objectives clearly.
- **Rewrite Activity 1 (Define the Purpose)** to instruct students to specify what decisions the concept test should inform (e.g., concept selection, design improvements, market potential). Provide examples of concept test questions (preference, improvement suggestions, purchase intent). Explain that these questions will guide the survey design.
- **Rewrite Activity 2 (Choose a Survey Population)** to guide students in identifying the target market segment for their hydroponic concept. Provide criteria (demographics, usage context, expertise). Explain how to determine a sample size using an online calculator and specify parameters like confidence level and margin of error. Encourage students to justify their choices.
- **Rewrite Activity 3 (Choose a Survey Format)** to explain different survey formats, their advantages and drawbacks, and how to choose a suitable format based on the product and population. Provide guidance on crafting clear and unbiased survey questions and structuring the survey (introduction, screening questions, concept evaluation questions, follow‑ups, demographics). Include sample questions.
- **Rewrite Activity 4 (Communicate the Concept)** to present criteria for selecting communication methods. Explain that simple concepts might be effectively conveyed with sketches or storyboards, while complex interactions may require videos or prototypes. Provide examples tailored to hydroponic systems (e.g., using a short video showing automatic nutrient adjustment). Instruct students to ensure that presentation formats align with chosen survey formats (e.g., sketches for mail surveys, videos for online surveys).
- **Rewrite in‑class activities** to have teams consolidate individual plans, agree on final purpose and research questions, finalise the survey population and sample size, select a survey format, draft survey questions collaboratively, and choose how to present the concept. Encourage peer review of questions for clarity and bias.
- **Add a concluding section** summarising the importance of concept testing, reminding students to document their plans and rationales, and explaining that the results will feed into concept refinement and final decision‑making.

### Activities / exercises
- **Concept test plan template**: Provide a template with fields for purpose, research questions, target market segment, sample size calculation (including parameters), survey format, communication method, and draft questions. Encourage students to complete this template individually and then refine it as a team.
- **Sample survey questions**: Include a list of generic concept test questions (preference, improvement suggestions, purchase intent, usage frequency, replacement/addition) that students can adapt to their hydroponic concepts.
- **Communication method decision matrix**: Provide a small matrix comparing communication methods (verbal description, sketches, photos, storyboards, videos, simulations, physical models) across criteria such as clarity, cost, and fidelity. Encourage students to use the matrix to select an appropriate method.
- **Pilot test exercise**: Suggest that students conduct a brief pilot of their survey with a few peers, collect feedback, and revise the questionnaire before fielding it to the selected population.
