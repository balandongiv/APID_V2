## Relevant Editorial Feedback (Mapped)
- **Missing learning objectives and context**: The chapter starts with tasks but does not explain what concept selection entails or why it is important. Explicit learning objectives should be added【708506183906356†L160-L169】.
- **Undefined terms**: “Selection criteria,” “concept‑screening matrix,” and “concept scoring” are mentioned without definitions or references to the design literature. Students need clarity on these tools and when to use them.
- **No link to earlier work**: The activities assume students have a set of concepts and customer needs but do not remind them to reference the outputs from Sessions 10 and 7. The screening matrix must align with the identified needs and specifications.
- **Lack of templates and examples**: Students are instructed to draw a table but are not given a template or sample matrix. Without examples, they may misinterpret the structure.
- **Missing evaluation criteria and scoring guidance**: The text instructs students to choose at least one dimension for selection but does not explain how to rate concepts, assign weights, or interpret results. The optional concept scoring activity is referenced but not explained.
- **No connection to physics or hydroponics**: There is no example showing how to select concepts for a hydroponic system or how physics principles influence selection criteria.
- **Grammatical issues and tone**: Several instructions use inconsistent grammar (“The objective of this activity to walk you through…”). The tone should be professional and consistent.
- **No conclusion or transition**: The chapter ends without summarising the selection process or guiding students to the next steps (e.g., concept refinement, prototyping).

## Action Plan (Prioritized)

### P0 – Must fix
- **Add learning objectives and introduction**: Begin by explaining what concept selection is (a process of evaluating and narrowing down concepts generated earlier) and list objectives: (1) identify selection criteria, (2) construct a concept‑screening matrix, (3) collaborate on selecting concepts, and (4) decide if more detailed scoring is needed.
- **Define key terms**: Provide concise definitions of selection criteria (attributes used to differentiate concepts), concept‑screening matrix (a tool for quickly comparing concepts against criteria), and concept scoring (a more detailed evaluation involving weighted scores). Reference the design literature or textbook definitions.
- **Provide a template for the screening matrix**: Include a sample matrix with concepts as columns and criteria as rows. Explain how to mark each cell (e.g., + if the concept is better than a reference concept, 0 if the same, – if worse) and how to compute net scores to rank concepts.
- **Explain how to derive selection criteria**: Instruct students to revisit customer needs and specifications, as well as enterprise considerations (e.g., cost, manufacturability), to choose criteria that effectively distinguish concepts. Provide examples relevant to hydroponics (e.g., nutrient delivery efficiency, cost, reliability, ease of maintenance).
- **Clarify the decision process for concept scoring**: Describe when concept scoring is appropriate (e.g., when screening results are too close or more resolution is needed) and outline the basic steps: selecting a datum, assigning weights to criteria, rating each concept, and calculating weighted scores.
- **Remove ambiguous or redundant text**: Correct grammar and unify instructions. Ensure each activity description is clear and complete.

### P1 – Should improve
- **Improve structure and readability**: Organise the chapter into sections: “Purpose and Learning Objectives,” “Generating Selection Criteria,” “Building the Concept‑Screening Matrix,” “In‑Class Consolidation,” and “Concept Scoring (Optional).” Provide explanatory text for each section.
- **Incorporate physics and hydroponic context**: Provide examples of selection criteria and matrices for the hydroponic concepts generated in Session 10. Highlight how physical principles (fluid dynamics, materials properties) and customer needs influence the criteria.
- **Offer guidance on collaboration and consensus**: Suggest techniques for teams to merge individual matrices (e.g., average scores, discussion) and reach consensus on selected concepts. Encourage respectful debate and evidence‑based decisions.
- **Provide evaluation criteria**: Suggest using a simple scoring system for screening (e.g., +/0/–) and a weighted scoring system for concept scoring. Explain how to interpret scores and rank concepts.
- **Add a conclusion**: Summarise the concept selection process and indicate that selected concepts will be further refined and potentially prototyped in subsequent sessions.

### P2 – Nice to have
- Provide examples or templates for concept scoring matrices with fictional data. Include guidance on setting weights based on importance to the customer and feasibility.
- Offer optional exercises where students practice concept screening on a simple example (e.g., selecting a smartphone design). This helps them understand the process before applying it to their project.
- Suggest reading or references on decision matrices and multi‑criteria decision analysis to broaden students’ understanding.
- Encourage reflection on biases and how to mitigate them during concept selection.

## Concrete Rewrite Instructions

### Chapter text
- **Insert an introduction** explaining concept selection and its role in narrowing down concepts generated in Session 10. State learning objectives clearly.
- **Add a subsection on generating selection criteria**: Explain how to derive criteria from customer needs (Session 7), specifications (Session 8), and enterprise needs. Provide examples relevant to the hydroponic system (e.g., nutrient delivery performance, ease of maintenance, cost). Encourage students to avoid unimportant criteria.
- **Define and explain the concept‑screening matrix**: Describe the +/0/– evaluation method relative to a reference concept. Provide a sample matrix with three concepts and four criteria. Explain how to calculate net scores and rank concepts.
- **Rewrite Activity 1** to guide students through generating selection criteria. Provide step‑by‑step instructions: review needs, select criteria, justify each criterion, and express criteria at a high level of abstraction.
- **Rewrite Activity 2** to instruct students on creating a concept‑screening matrix. Include instructions on using a datum concept (e.g., a baseline concept from Session 10) and marking each cell with +, 0, or –. Instruct students to calculate net scores (sum of +1/0/–1) and rank concepts.
- **Rewrite in‑class Activity 1** to describe how teams should collect individual matrices, discuss differences, and merge them into a consensus matrix. Suggest using average scores or discussion to resolve disagreements.
- **Rewrite Activity 2** (Deciding on the Need for Concept Scoring)**: Explain what concept scoring involves and when to employ it. Provide a brief outline: (1) select a datum, (2) assign weights to criteria based on importance, (3) rate each concept (e.g., 1–5 scale), and (4) compute weighted scores. Encourage teams to justify their decision to proceed or not proceed with scoring.
- **Add a closing section** summarising the concept selection process, reminding students to document chosen concepts and rationale, and previewing the next steps (e.g., concept refinement, prototyping).
