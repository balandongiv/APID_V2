# Revision Report – Session 3 / Innovation Charter and Opportunity Identification (Part 1)

## Chapter Overview
| Item | Description |
|---|---|
| **Chapter file(s)** | `session3/session3.tex` |
| **Related/shared files** | Uses global macros and packages from `main.tex`. |
| **Current chapter purpose** | To guide students through generating and refining an innovation charter for their group project and to introduce the concept of opportunity identification. The pre‑class activity asks each member to draft multiple innovation charters with broad scope, and the in‑class activity requires the team to discuss, combine and refine these charters into a single statement. |
| **Intended student takeaway** | Students should understand what an innovation charter is, why it is important for guiding the scope of product development, how to brainstorm and write multiple charters with broad boundaries, and how to combine or select charters collaboratively. |
| **Audience‑specific risks** | Without context, third‑year Industrial Physics students may not appreciate why an innovation charter matters or how broad scope can foster innovation. The chapter lacks learning objectives and a clear definition of “innovation charter.” Activities rely on students’ intuition without providing examples. Transitions between pre‑class and in‑class activities are minimal, and instructions such as “assume that there are several innovation chart” contain grammatical errors that may confuse readers. |

## Relevant Editorial Feedback (Mapped)
- **No definition of innovation charter** – The term is introduced without explanation. Students unfamiliar with product development terminology need a concise definition and rationale.
- **Missing learning objectives and overview** – The chapter does not state what students should learn or why the tasks are valuable, reducing engagement.
- **Ambiguous instructions** – Phrases like “draft one or more innovation charters that you believe will be advantageous” lack guidance on length, format or content. The instruction “Assume that there are several innovation chart” is grammatically incorrect and unclear.
- **No examples** – Without sample charters, students may struggle to understand the desired breadth and depth. Including one or two examples (e.g., the FroliCat charter) would help.
- **Weak connection to applied physics** – The text does not relate innovation charters to the hydroponics context or applied physics principles. Students may not see the relevance of the exercise.
- **Limited signposting and transitions** – There is no concluding section summarising what students should achieve before the next session.

## Action Plan (Prioritized)

### P0 – Must fix
- **Define “innovation charter”** up front. Explain that it is a concise statement capturing the goals and boundaries of an innovation effort and serves as a compass throughout product development.
- **Add learning objectives** – E.g., “By the end of this session you should be able to (1) describe the purpose of an innovation charter; (2) draft multiple charters with broad scope; and (3) collaboratively select or synthesise a charter that fits your project.”
- **Clarify instructions** – Provide step‑by‑step guidance on drafting charters: recommend length (one or two sentences), suggest content (problem statement, target category, time frame) and emphasise broad scope.
- **Correct grammar and phrasing** – Fix errors such as “innovation chart” (should be “innovation charter”) and ensure sentences have subjects and verbs.

### P1 – Should improve
- **Provide context and examples** – Include the FroliCat example (already quoted) and at least one more example relevant to hydroponics or industrial physics. Highlight how charters differ in scope and ambition.
- **Explain why broad scope is beneficial** – Clarify that a broader charter encourages exploration of diverse opportunities and reduces premature narrowing. Cite research or guidelines if available.
- **Guide the charter selection process** – Suggest criteria for evaluating charters (alignment with team interests, feasibility, relevance to physics, potential impact). Encourage discussion of pros and cons before combining or selecting.
- **Add signposting** – Introduce each activity with a brief description of its purpose and summarise expected outputs. Close the chapter with a “Next Steps” section instructing students to finalise their charter and prepare for opportunity generation in Session 4.
- **Connect to physics and hydroponics** – Explain that the innovation charter should frame a product opportunity within the hydroponics theme and highlight relevant physics considerations (e.g., control of nutrient flows, energy efficiency). This reinforces discipline relevance.

### P2 – Nice to have
- **Develop a charter template** – Provide a fill‑in‑the‑blank template or checklist (e.g., product category, time frame, target users) to help students structure their draft charters.
- **Include a peer‑review step** – Encourage students to share and critique each other’s charters before the in‑class discussion, fostering critical evaluation skills.
- **Offer optional reading** – Suggest sections from product development textbooks that explain innovation charters and opportunity identification.

## Concrete Rewrite Instructions

### Chapter text
- **Add a “Session Overview and Objectives” section** at the beginning. Define the innovation charter and explain its role in product development. State the learning objectives clearly.
- **Rewrite the pre‑class instructions** to provide guidance: instruct each student to draft at least two innovation charters, each one to two sentences long, that articulate a broad problem statement, potential product category and time frame. Emphasise that charters should not specify solutions, allowing freedom to explore opportunities.
- **Clarify the example** – Present the FroliCat charter in a box or quote and explain why it is effective (focuses on category and time frame, not specific technology).
- **Replace ambiguous sentences** such as “Assume that there are several innovation chart” with clear directives: “After drafting, review your charters and refine them. Bring your top two charters to class to share with your team.”
- **Expand the in‑class activity** – Instruct teams to compare individual charters, discuss their merits using criteria such as relevance to hydroponics, potential impact, feasibility and alignment with physics principles. Encourage combining elements from different charters if appropriate. Direct the team to agree on one charter or synthesise a new one.
- **Conclude with a summary** – In a “Next Steps” subsection, tell students to document the final charter, upload it to the course platform and use it as a compass for generating opportunities in Session 4.

### Activities / exercises
- **Add evaluation criteria** – Provide a short list of questions students should ask when reviewing charters (e.g., Does this charter inspire a range of opportunities? Does it avoid prescribing specific solutions? Does it connect to the hydroponics theme and relevant physics?). Encourage students to justify their selection.
- **Encourage peer feedback** – Suggest that after drafting, students exchange charters for constructive feedback before the in‑class session.

### Figures / tables / captions
- **Include an example charter in a shaded box or call‑out** to draw attention. Label it appropriately and reference it in the text.

### Terminology / notation / units
- **Ensure consistent use of the term “innovation charter”** and avoid abbreviations. Define new terms (e.g., “scope,” “boundary conditions”) if used.

### LaTeX / implementation notes
- **Use `\section` and `\subsection`** headings appropriately to structure the chapter. Avoid burying important definitions inside bullet lists.
- **Add a call‑out environment** (e.g., `tcolorbox`) for example charters if the class file supports it. Ensure that this compiles and matches the style of other call‑outs.
