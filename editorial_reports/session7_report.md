## Relevant Editorial Feedback (Mapped)
- **Missing learning objectives and context**: The chapter dives straight into activities without explaining why identifying customer needs is critical for product design or how the activities relate to prior sessions. Adding learning objectives at the beginning will clarify purpose【708506183906356†L160-L169】.
- **Unclear definitions**: Terms such as “customer statement,” “needs statement,” “lead users,” “extreme users,” and “variable type” are used without explanation. Provide definitions or link to a glossary.
- **Poor organisation and heavy cognitive load**: Instructions are presented in long paragraphs and enumerations. Breaking content into subheadings (e.g., “Survey Question Generation,” “Needs Translation,” “Lead and Extreme Users”) with short explanatory text will improve readability.
- **Lack of guidance for survey design**: Students are told to brainstorm questions but are not reminded of the hallmarks of good survey questions (clarity, bias avoidance, alignment with objectives). Provide criteria and examples.
- **Limited connection to physics and hydroponics**: The chapter uses generic examples (screwdriver, thermostat) unrelated to the hydroponic product studied in this course. Replace or supplement them with examples relevant to Industrial Physics and hydroponics.
- **Table inconsistencies**: The tables for customer statements and needs (thermostat example and long table for interpreted needs) have inconsistent column widths and unclear captions. Provide a standard table template and ensure variable types are defined.
- **Lead/extreme user activity unclear**: The instructions ask students to reflect on the need to interview lead/extreme users but do not explain who these users are or why they matter. Include definitions and examples.
- **Optional conceptual question**: The prompt on how needs identification could lead to innovative concepts is tucked under “optional” without guidance. Provide context and encourage critical thinking rather than leaving students unsure.
- **Missing conclusion**: The chapter ends abruptly after the table. A closing section should summarise outcomes and preview how needs feed into product specifications (Session 8).

## Action Plan (Prioritized)

### P0 – Must fix
- **Add learning objectives and introduction**: Clearly state that students will (1) generate effective survey questions, (2) translate customer statements into needs statements, (3) understand the roles of lead and extreme users, and (4) compile needs in a structured format. Explain that identifying customer needs guides the development of product specifications and innovative concepts.
- **Define key terms**: Provide concise definitions for customer statements, needs statements, lead users (those who experience needs earlier than the mainstream), extreme users (those at the edges of use cases), and variable types (functional, usability, cost, etc.). Use or update the global glossary.
- **Standardise table format**: Provide a clear table template with columns for question/prompt, customer statement, interpreted need, variable type, and (optionally) need type (direct, latent, constant, variable, general, niche). Ensure captions describe what the table shows.
- **Clarify the activity deliverables**: Specify what students need to submit (e.g., three survey questions with justifications, a table of customer statements and needs, an analysis of lead/extreme user inclusion, and responses to the optional conceptual question).
- **Explain lead/extreme user importance**: Define these user types and explain why interviewing them can reveal latent or advanced needs, thus leading to more innovative concepts.

### P1 – Should improve
- **Improve organisation and readability**: Divide the chapter into subsections with headings such as “Survey Question Generation,” “Needs Statement Generation,” “Lead and Extreme User Consideration,” and “Linking Needs to Innovation.” Provide brief explanatory text before each set of instructions.
- **Provide survey design guidelines and examples**: Remind students that survey questions should be clear, unbiased, and aligned with objectives. Offer example questions relevant to hydroponics (e.g., “How frequently do you adjust nutrient levels in your hydroponic system?”) and explain why they are effective.
- **Use relevant examples**: Replace or supplement the screwdriver and thermostat examples with a hydroponic product example. For instance, show how a customer complaint (“I never know when to refill the nutrient reservoir”) translates to a need (“The system provides clear indications of nutrient levels”).
- **Explicitly describe variable types**: Provide a short description of each variable type (functional, usability, cost, energy saving, connectivity, etc.) and instruct students to label interpreted needs accordingly.
- **Encourage inclusion of lead and extreme users**: Provide scenarios illustrating how insights from early adopters or users with atypical needs can inspire innovative features (e.g., extreme users of hydroponic systems in arid climates).
- **Clarify the optional conceptual question**: Frame the reflective question on how needs identification can lead to innovative concepts as a short discussion prompt or journal entry, and provide guiding questions (e.g., “What unexpected need could lead to a breakthrough product like the Post-it note?”).
- **Add a conclusion**: Summarise the key activities, remind students to compile their needs tables, and explain that these needs will become the basis for metrics in Session 8.

### P2 – Nice to have
- Provide links to articles or videos on survey design and customer interviewing.
- Introduce techniques such as empathy mapping or journey mapping to help students think from the customer’s perspective.
- Suggest using software or spreadsheet tools to capture and organise customer statements and needs.
- Encourage students to reflect on biases and how to avoid them when translating statements into needs.

## Concrete Rewrite Instructions

### Chapter text
- **Add an introductory section** at the start of the chapter that explains the role of customer needs in product development. State learning objectives clearly and describe how the activities connect to the mission statement created previously and the specifications to be developed next.
- **Define key terms** in the text or refer to a shared glossary. For example, “A **customer statement** is a direct quote or paraphrase describing a user’s experience or frustration. A **needs statement** rephrases that statement into a concise description of what the product must do.” Provide definitions for lead and extreme users.
- **Restructure the pre‑class activities** into separate subsections:
  - *Survey Question Generation*: Introduce survey design principles (clarity, neutrality, alignment with objectives) and instruct students to generate at least three questions. Provide a table or bullet list for recording questions and justifications.
  - *Needs Statement Generation*: Explain the process of observing or imagining product use, gathering customer statements, and translating them into needs statements. Provide a hydroponic example.
  - *Lead and Extreme User Consideration*: Define these user types and ask students to analyse whether interviewing them is beneficial. Provide guiding questions for reflection.
  - *Optional Reflection*: Frame the innovation question as a reflective discussion and encourage students to provide short written responses.
- **Revise the in‑class activity description** to instruct teams to merge individual questions and needs statements into a consolidated table. Provide guidance on grouping needs by themes (e.g., typical use, likes, dislikes, improvements) and on using LLMs as optional tools, with reminders to document prompts.
- **Move long examples to figures**: Present the thermostat example as a figure or table in an appendix or as an illustrative example, and replace it with a hydroponic‑related example in the main text.
- **Add a closing subsection** summarising what students should submit and how the compiled needs will be used in Session 8 to generate product specifications.

### Activities / exercises
- **Survey Question Table**: Introduce a template for recording survey questions, response options (if applicable), and justifications. Include an example row relevant to hydroponics.
- **Needs Translation Table**: Provide a standard template with columns: Customer Statement, Needs Statement, Variable Type (Functional, Usability, Cost, etc.), (optional) Need Type (Direct, Latent, Constant, Variable, General, Niche). Provide a sample row using the hydroponic example.
- **Lead and Extreme User Analysis**: Ask students to summarise their reasoning in a paragraph or bullet points, citing any sources consulted.
- **Innovation Reflection**: Encourage short written responses discussing how identifying unmet or latent needs can lead to novel product concepts.

- **Replace generic examples**: Include a hydroponic example table and provide context in the caption.
- **Ensure all figures/images** have descriptive captions and are referenced in the text. Remove commented‑out figures if not needed.

- Avoid abbreviations without explanation (e.g., LLM). If large language models are mentioned, define them and clarify their optional role.
